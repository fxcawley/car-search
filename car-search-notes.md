# Used Car Search Notes — March 2026

## Background

This document consolidates a multi-day used car search conducted in late March 2026. The buyer is shopping for a reliable daily driver in the **$3,000–$6,000** price range, primarily looking at listings in **Michigan, Ohio, Indiana, and Illinois**. Each vehicle was evaluated by pulling its Carfax report, analyzing the ownership chain, identifying known mechanical issues for that platform, and assessing the risk-to-value ratio. The buyer prioritized:

- **Clean service history** (ideally dealer-documented)
- **No or minimal accident history**
- **Mechanically simple / proven drivetrains**
- **Low total cost of ownership** (parts + labor)
- **Rust belt survivability** (all candidates are Midwest cars)

Eighteen vehicles were evaluated in total. Each section below contains the full Carfax summary, known platform issues, a red/yellow/green assessment, and the final verdict.

**About mechanical condition:**
- "Does it consume oil between changes? If so, how much?"
- "Any check engine lights, fault codes, or stored codes?" (Ask them to pull codes)
- "How does the transmission shift? Any shudder at highway speed or harsh 2→3 shifts?"
- "Any noise from the transfer case in tight turns?"
- "Has the water pump ever been replaced?"

**About the accident:**
- "The 2012 accident has no details — do you have any information?"
- "Can I have it put on a frame rack / measured for alignment symmetry?"

### Test Drive Checklist
- **Cold start:** Listen for timing chain rattle or ticking (first 10 seconds)
- **Idle:** Watch for rough idle or fluctuating RPM (VANOS)
- **Highway:** Feel for torque converter shudder at 40–60 mph under light throttle
- **Parking lot:** Full-lock turns both directions — listen for grinding/clunking (transfer case)
- **Braking:** Any pulsation or pulling
- **Dipstick:** Correct level? Dark/sludgy?
- **Coolant reservoir:** Correct level, no oil film, no sweet smell from exhaust

### Verdict

**Ownership / history:**
- "How long have you owned it? Are you the original owner?"
- "Why are you selling?"
- "Do you have service records or receipts?"
- "Has it ever been in an accident?"

**Mechanical — deal-breakers:**
- "Has the clutch ever been replaced? Does it slip in high gear under load?"
- "Any oil consumption between changes? How much?"
- "Has the head gasket ever been done? Any coolant loss or overheating?"
- "Any check engine light on? Recently cleared?"

**Mechanical — wear items:**
- "Any humming or droning at highway speed?" (wheel bearings)
- "Any clicking when turning?" (CV joints)
- "When were spark plugs last done?"

**Condition / rust:**

### Go / No-Go Criteria

**Buy if:**
- No head gasket issues (no coolant loss, no milky oil, no overheating history)
- Clutch engages cleanly (even if replacement needed soon — known cost)
- No frame/subframe rust-through
- No grinding/whining from transmission or differential
- Owner seems straightforward
- Check engine light is off

**Walk away if:**
- Sweet smell from exhaust or bubbles in coolant reservoir (head gasket)
- Milky residue on oil cap
- Clutch slips AND owner claims it's "fine" (dishonesty signal)
- Structural rust on subframe or brake lines
- Transmission grinds going into any gear (synchros — expensive on manual Subaru)
- He won't let you take it to a mechanic

### Negotiation
$5k is a bit high for 190k private party. If the clutch is original, use that as leverage: **"$4,000 cash, because I'm looking at a clutch job in the near future."**

### Verdict
The **lowest-risk drivetrain** of all candidates. The manual transmission + NA boxer engine is fundamentally simpler and cheaper to maintain than anything else in this search. The BMW has better documentation, but the Subaru has a fundamentally simpler and cheaper-to-maintain drivetrain. **The phone call and test drive are your Carfax** on this one.

### The CVT Is the Elephant in the Room
Same FB-series engine family as the Impreza, but the **CVT changes the calculus significantly:**
- CVT failure is the #1 catastrophic risk: shudder, jerking, sudden loss of power, whining
- **Replacement cost: $3,500–$6,000+** (often exceeds the car's value at 192k)
- Subaru extended CVT warranty to 10 years / 100,000 miles due to widespread issues — this car is well past both
- CVT fluid changes every 30k are critical. If skipped, the CVT is on borrowed time.

### What to Ask on the Phone

**CVT-specific (most important):**
- "Has the CVT fluid ever been changed? How many times? Do you have receipts?"
- "Any shuddering, hesitation, or jerking when accelerating from a stop?"
- "Any whining or droning that changes with speed but NOT with engine RPM?" (CVT bearing failure)
- "Has the CVT ever been rebuilt, replaced, or had the valve body serviced?"

**Same FB-series questions as the Impreza:**
- Oil consumption, overheating/coolant loss, check engine light, service records

**Condition:**
- "Listing says no rust — can I see the subframe and brake lines?"
- "Tires — how old are they, and do they match?" (mismatched tires kill AWD systems)

### Go / No-Go

**Buy if:**
- CVT fluid changed at least 2–3 times with receipts
- No shudder/hesitation on test drive (especially 0–30 mph)
- No head gasket symptoms
- Clean undercarriage

**Walk away if:**
- CVT fluid never changed (or owner doesn't know) — near-certain future failure
- Any shudder, hesitation, or RPM flare without corresponding acceleration
- Whining from the transmission area

### Head-to-Head: Impreza Manual vs. Outback CVT

| Factor | Impreza 5-speed ($5,000) | Outback CVT ($5,900) |
|--------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| Transmission risk | Near zero (manual) | High (CVT @ 192k) |
| Worst-case repair | Clutch $1k / head gasket $2k | CVT $4k–$6k / head gasket $2k |
| Comfort/features | Base-ish | Limited trim — leather, heated seats, camera |
| Cargo | Small hatch | Full wagon — much more practical |

### Verdict
The Outback is the nicer car, but the CVT at 192k is a fundamentally different risk profile than a manual transmission. **No CVT maintenance receipts = walk.** The Impreza manual remains the lower-risk buy.

## General Buying Advice

### Pre-Purchase Inspection (PPI)
**Non-negotiable on any of these cars.** $100–$150 at an independent specialist (BMW shop for the BMWs, etc.). Specifically request:
- Compression/leak-down test if oil consumption is suspected
- Scan for stored and pending fault codes
- Visual inspection underneath for rust, leaks, and structural damage
- Transmission behavior assessment

### Test Drive Checklist (Universal)
1. **Cold start** — listen for rattles, ticking, rough idle in the first 10–30 seconds
2. **Idle** — watch for fluctuating RPM, unusual vibration
3. **0–30 mph acceleration** — feel for transmission shudder, hesitation, or jerking
4. **Highway speed (40–60 mph)** — light throttle, feel for torque converter shudder
5. **Parking lot turns** — full lock both directions, listen for grinding/clunking (AWD systems)
6. **Braking** — any pulsation, pulling, or noise
7. **Fluid checks** — oil dipstick (level, color, sludge), coolant reservoir (level, oil film), under oil cap (milky residue = head gasket)

### Negotiation Framework
- Know the CARFAX/KBB fair value before you call
- Use documented deferred maintenance as leverage ("water pump hasn't been done — that's $800 I need to budget")
- At this price range, **cash talks** — lead with "I'll pay $X,XXX cash today"
- Small dealers and private sellers are more negotiable than franchise dealers

### Rust Belt Survival Priorities
All of these cars have spent years in salt-belt states. Priority inspection areas:
1. **Frame/subframe** — structural rust is a safety issue and an instant walk-away
2. **Brake lines** — corroded steel brake lines can rupture
3. **Rocker panels** — cosmetic but indicates overall rust severity
4. **Rear wheel arches** — common rust-through point
5. **Exhaust system** — if not already replaced
