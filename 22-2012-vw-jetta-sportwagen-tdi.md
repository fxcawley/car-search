## The Dieselgate Story on This Car

VW installed defeat device software in the EA189 TDI that cheated emissions tests. This car was part of the scandal:

1. **Lease returned to VW** -- car went to Auburn Hills, MI (VW's US HQ area) at 46,704 mi in Oct 2017
2. **Emissions fix applied** 01/2018 at 46,706 mi -- "Diesel Emission System Has Been Reported as Repaired and in Compliance with Federal Government Standards"
3. **VW resold it** -- Owner 2 bought it in Ferndale, MI (Feb 2018)

The Dieselgate emissions fix is known to cause:
- Reduced performance and fuel economy (some owners report)
- Increased DPF clogging issues
- Increased DEF consumption
- Some owners report no issues at all

This car has 135,000 miles on the post-fix engine. If the fix caused problems, they'd likely have appeared by now.

## The Showstopper: Timing Belt

**The EA189 is an interference engine with a timing belt.** VW specifies replacement at **120,000 miles**. This car has **182,064 miles**. There is **zero documentation** that the timing belt was ever changed.

If the timing belt snaps on an interference engine:
- Pistons collide with valves
- Valves bend
- Possible piston damage
- **Engine is destroyed**
- Replacement cost: $3,000--$6,000+

Even if someone changed it at ~120k (undocumented), you have no way to verify. **You cannot safely drive this car until the timing belt status is confirmed or replaced.**

Timing belt replacement cost (preventive): **$800--$1,200** (belt, tensioner, water pump, idler -- always do the water pump at the same time).

## The TDI Paradox

The EA189 2.0L TDI is genuinely a legendary engine. When properly maintained:
- 40--45 MPG highway
- 236 lb-ft of torque (more than the N52 or FB20)
- Known to run 300,000--400,000 miles
- Wagon body = practical daily driver
- Diesel fuel economy saves real money over time

But "properly maintained" is the operative phrase. This car has the worst service documentation of anything in the search -- worse than the ML320, worse than the CR-V. At least the ML320 has a non-interference engine and California provenance. This car has an interference engine at 60,000 miles past its timing belt interval with no proof the belt was changed.

## What Would Make This Worth Buying

1. **Price under $3,000** -- to account for the immediate $800-$1,200 timing belt job
2. **Timing belt receipt from Owner 3 or 4** -- ask the seller directly
3. **Manual transmission** -- eliminates the DSG risk ($2,000-$4,000 potential failure)
4. **Pre-purchase inspection at a TDI specialist** -- compression test, turbo check, DPF status, timing belt visual inspection

## Verdict

**Pass.** The TDI engine is legendary, but this particular car is the embodiment of "great engine, terrible history." 182,000 miles with effectively zero maintenance documentation on an interference engine that needs a timing belt at 120k is an unacceptable risk. Four owners, an accident, and the Dieselgate fix add to the uncertainty.

If the price is under $3,000 and you can verify the timing belt was done (or budget $1,200 to do it immediately), it becomes a cautious maybe. The wagon body and diesel economy are genuinely appealing. But without timing belt verification, **you are gambling that a rubber belt 60,000+ miles past its replacement interval hasn't degraded to the point of failure.**

For comparison, the ML320 on BaT at $2,900 has a non-interference engine (no timing belt catastrophe risk), California provenance (no rust), and lower miles (107k vs 182k). The Schererville 328i at $4,800 has a timing chain (not belt), all-dealer service, and zero accidents. Both are safer bets.
